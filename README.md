## 📚 Course Overview

**The Culture of Business, Finance and the Economy** is an interdisciplinary course that explores how business, finance, and economics operate not only as technical disciplines, but as cultural systems.

Through case studies ranging from management consultants and financial analysts to venture capitalists and economists, the course examines:

- How economic expertise is constructed and legitimized  
- How models, numbers, and quantification shape reality  
- How ideas like “efficiency” or “markets” become common sense  
- How professional cultures (consultants, analysts, entrepreneurs) reproduce authority  
- How economics presents itself as culture-free — and why that claim itself is cultural  

Rather than “debunking” economics or business, the course seeks to understand **how and why certain ways of thinking came to dominate**.

---

## 🎯 Course Objectives

By the end of the course, students should be able to:

- Understand business, finance, and economics as cultural systems
- Analyze how expertise and authority are produced
- Critically examine economic models and quantification practices
- Reflect on how economic reasoning shapes social life
- Identify the cultural foundations of technocracy and professional managerial power

The course is designed for students from multiple disciplines and does **not** require prior background in economics.

## 🧠 Core Themes

- The cultural foundations of economics  
- The mathematization of economic reasoning  
- Models as ways of knowing  
- Consultant cultures and professional abstraction  
- Financial analysts and narrative construction  
- The entrepreneurial self  
- Venture capital and start-up culture  
- Corporate culture and corporate social responsibility  
- Meritocracy and technocracy

## 🗂 Program Structure

The course consists of approximately **20–24 classes**, organized as follows:

1. Introduction: What is the Culture of Business?
2. The Superiority of Economists
3. Spontaneous Order and Economists as Shamans
4. The Invention of Mathematics
5. The Mathematization of Economic Reasoning
6. Economics Copies Physics
7. Models as a Way of Knowing
8. Thinking Like an Economist
9. Numbers and Quantification
10–13. Consultant Cultures (Origins, Legitimacy, Rhetoric, Uncertainty)
14–15. Financial Analysts
16–17. The Entrepreneurial Self
18–19. Start-Up Culture and Venture Capitalism
20. Venture Capitalists
21. Corporate Culture
22. Corporate Social Responsibility
23. Meritocracy and Entrepreneurialism
24. Technocracy and the Rule of Experts

Minor adjustments may occur depending on academic calendar and class dynamics.
