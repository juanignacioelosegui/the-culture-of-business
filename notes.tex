        \subsection*{Class I - 03.03}
            \noindent \textbf{What is ``The Culture of Business, Finance, and the Economy''?} \\
            \noindent This course examines the \textbf{practices, intuitions, and less-examined kinds of knowledge} that make business, finance, and the economy function in a particular way. It draws on case studies ranging from Norwegian management consultants and Swiss investment bankers to medieval traders and Native North American shamans. \\
            \\
            \noindent \textbf{Guiding questions:}
            \begin{itemize}
                \item What kinds of expertise do consultants actually have and where does their authority come from?
                \item How do finance experts come to know the stock market — and their successes and failures in it?
                \item How do abstract notions like ``efficiency'' travel from economic theory to popular intuitions of how markets work?
                \item What kinds of technologies — gadgets, materials, ways of knowing — produce business, financial or economic expertise as something unique and authoritative? \\
            \end{itemize}

            \noindent \textbf{Objective:} \\
            Examine the ways in which business, finance and the economy work, think, and make sense of things \emph{beyond the claims these professions and disciplines make about themselves}. The aim is not to antagonise or ``debunk'' them, but rather to reflect on:
            \begin{itemize}
                \item Why certain ways of doing things stuck instead of others;
                \item How specific ideas came to mean what they do and with what consequences;
                \item How certain technologies and ways of knowing can shape the things they purport to merely describe.
            \end{itemize}

            \noindent This way of thinking and of doing things is in itself a \textbf{culture} that requires as much learning, nurturing, reproducing and ritualising as any other culture does. \\
            \\
            \noindent \textbf{What is culture?}
            \begin{itemize}
                \item Culture is a \textbf{way of seeing things}, a \textbf{way of thinking}, a \textbf{way of making sense}.
                \item Culture influences how we experience things, how we think about, apprehend and make sense of complex notions like normality and deviance, the good and the bad, correctness and impropriety; how we ``sort through'' what matters and does not matter.
                \item Cultures are \textbf{learned and mediated by artefacts, technologies and knowledge} that reproduce it, sustain it, and make it. \\
            \end{itemize}
            
            \noindent \textbf{Technologies and ways of knowing in TCBFE:} \\
            All of these technologies, artefacts and ways of knowing create:
            \begin{itemize}
                \item A way of making sense of the world;
                \item A way of ordering the things we encounter;
                \item A way of deciding what matters and what not, when, and for what;
                \item A way of persuading ourselves and trying to persuade others;
                \item A way of generating a common sense and an intuition. \\
            \end{itemize}
            
            \noindent \textbf{How to study TCBFE:} \\
            Examining TCBFE from the outside requires us to generate such a thing as an ``outside'' from which to make sense of it — to understand how ``a bunch of lines'' becomes a commonsensical proposition about the workings of our world.
        \subsection*{Class II - 05.03}
            \noindent According to the reading \textit{The Superiority of Economics}, economics is \textbf{seen as superior} not only intellectually but also professionally, politically, economically and morally. \\
            This superiority is linked to strong reliance on formal, mathematical methods; high market demand, tight disciplinary control, and high compensation. \\
            \\
            \noindent But, why is it \emph{superior}?
            \begin{itemize}
                \item \textbf{Insularity.} Economics is highly self-referential: most citations are within economics itself, and economists are not likely to engage with other disciplines.
                \item \textbf{Method.} They justify their superiority through mathematical models, abstraction and simplification. This leads to viewing other sciences as less rigorous and expand to other fields using tools such as game theory, rational choice theory and general equilibrium models.
                \item \textbf{Disciplinary Control.} here is a tight control over what counts as ``proper economics''. Academic departments, top journals, and PhD programs enforce a unified orthodoxy (often neoclassical), filtering out heterodox approaches.
                \item \textbf{Market Demand \& Prestige.} Economists hold \emph{key} positions in governments, central banks, and international organizations. Their expertise is highly valued in the private sector (consulting, finance), granting them significant political and economic power.
                \item \textbf{Moral Authority.} Economics presents itself as a ``science'' that can objectively determine the most efficient outcomes, often framing policy choices as technical necessities rather than political decisions, which gives it a moral high ground.
            \end{itemize}
        \subsection*{Class III - 10.03 (Economists as Shamans)}
            \noindent \textbf{What is a shaman?} \\
            \noindent Shamans mediate between worlds. They act on behalf of the community, or of individuals, healing, harming, translating and interpreting knowledge, mediating and producing obscure, secret or forbidden knowledge. They translate the unseen, engaging the gods, the world, nature and anything beyond "normal" human abilities through techniques and knowledge only they have, artefacts only they are allowed or effectively able to use. \\
            \\
            \noindent The term \emph{shamanism} comes from the Manchu Tungus word \textit{šaman}. The noun is formed from the verb \textit{ša-} ‘to know’; a shaman is \textbf{“one who knows.”} \\
            \\
            \noindent \textbf{Rites of Passage} \\
            \noindent They are the culturally approved and culturally understood moments where one's status changes: one becomes something else. These moments test us, push us to a certain limit, sometimes defile or degrade us, to mark us as able to handle, produce, or mediate certain kinds of knowledge or practices. To make it past them is to become worthy of that which is kept beyond that test. \\
            \\
            \noindent \textbf{Example: Investment banking interns}
            \begin{itemize}
                \item Highly competitive environment: fighting 1500 candidates for one of two places.
                \item Brutal hours: working through the night is almost a \textbf{rite of passage}.
                \item The ``magic roundabout'': taxi home at 5AM, shower, put on a fresh shirt, return to desk.
                \item Rules against overwork are impossible to implement because junior staff will work around policies to prove themselves.
                \item As a human resources consultant put it: ``Stating that one should not work on Saturdays is like telling people in a bar that they should drink less. In other words, there is a culture and there are rules. If these rules do not reflect the culture, rules are useless.''
            \end{itemize}

            \noindent \textbf{Why does this matter?} The market as an order — not whether ``it works'' or not, but what matters is the \emph{kind of sense it makes} when we think about it in these terms. The culture of economics involves:
            \begin{itemize}
                \item A particular way of understanding order (spontaneous, immanent)
                \item A particular figure of authority (the economist as shaman, ``one who knows'')
                \item Particular rituals and tests (rites of passage that produce legitimate knowers)
            \end{itemize}
        \subsection*{Class IV - 12.03}
            \noindent \textbf{Mathematics: from divine perfection to a deal with Satan.} \\
            \noindent How did mathematics become what it is today, and how did it become part of economics? Was it always ``clear'', ``objective'', ``perfect''? Was it a fully developed tool or logic? Did we discover it or did we invent it as a universal means of knowledge? \\
            \\
            \noindent \textbf{Mathematics in the 1600s: Bacon vs. words} \\
            \noindent In 1623, Francis Bacon argued that words ``do violence to the understanding'' and that we needed ``a new and deeper remedy.'' The question was: where was that new remedy? \\
            \\
            \noindent \textbf{What we call mathematics did not exist back then} \\
            \noindent The heart of mathematics was \textbf{geometry}. When Galileo stated that God wrote the book of the world in the language of mathematics, he was referring to \emph{geometric diagrams}, not to symbolic notation. The Seven Liberal Arts included Grammar, Logic, Rhetoric, Arithmetic, Geometry, Music, and Astrology --- but not ``mathematics'' in the modern sense. \\
            \noindent Descartes held that Arithmetic and Geometry were \emph{free from any taint of falsity or uncertainty} because they derived knowledge through deduction, not from experience. Western culture at the time devalued ``mechanical'' activities such as calculation. \\
            \\
            \noindent \textbf{The limits of Geometry} \\
            \noindent In Geometry, quantities (``four apples'') and magnitudes (four square meters) were different \emph{kinds} of numbers. So the equation: \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \) did not even make sense, because it ``pretends'' to add linear and square kinds. \\
            \noindent \textbf{Alogos.} Since Euclid and the beginnings of Geometry, everyone knew of lines whose lengths cannot be expressed as multiples of a common unit (e.g., $\sqrt{2}$). These irrational numbers posed a \emph{deep conceptual problem}. \\
            \\
            \noindent \textbf{Proto-algebra: a plebeian practice} \\
            \noindent A proto-algebra existed, but it was considered dirty, plebeian, and \emph{not real mathematics}. It was not one of the traditional liberal arts and was perceived to lack rigorous standards of proof. Algebra was a practical, earthly kind of knowledge associated with trivial merchant, sailing, and trade-related calculations. \\
            \noindent \textbf{Al-Khw\=arizm\=\i} wrote one of the foundational texts of algebra in 820 AD. The word \emph{algebra} itself derives from \textit{al-jabr}, usually translated as ``completion.'' His proto-algebra was written entirely in prose --- describing step by step how to carry and place digits, use zeros, and perform operations. \\
            \noindent Al-Khw\=arizm\=\i{} knew about irrational numbers such as $\sqrt{2}$; he instructed the reader that one must be content with ``an approximation, and not the exact truth: for God alone knows what the exact root is.'' \\
            \noindent This proto-algebra spread across Europe through \emph{merchants}, not through philosophers. It was primarily the business of ``mathematical practitioners'': people who taught mathematics outside of the university system through textbooks, lecturing, and tutoring. Its methods had practical applications in navigation, trade, and other disciplines. \\
            \\
            \textbf{Conflicting ideas about numbers} \\
            \noindent Algebra brought with it \emph{conflicting ideas} about what numbers are and how they work. \textbf{Stifel} wrote en 1544 that it was disputed whether irrational numbers were true numbers or fictions. In their favor was their utility in calculation; against them was the fact that when we try to express them as rational numbers, ``we find that they flee perpetually.'' \\
            \noindent A major shift in the conception of number occurred: numbers began to be imagined not through the perfection of arithmetic and geometry, \emph{but through the digits themselves} --- the tools of the commoners who practiced algebra. The claim emerged that ``there are no absurd, irrational, irregular, inexplicable numbers'': one cannot write $\sqrt{2}$ as a fraction, but one can write it as $1.4142\ldots$ and continue the expansion as far as one likes. \\
            \noindent Even further: cubic equations, absurd for Euclidean geometry, yielded roots of negative numbers. $\sqrt{-1}$ cannot even be approximated by decimal fractions. For centuries this was rejected as a sign that the equation had no solution, yet the fact that algebra's procedures could produce such results seemed to imply that the procedures themselves were ill-founded. \\
            \\
            \textbf{Algebra enters mathematics}\\
            \noindent \textbf{Fran\c{c}ois Vi\`ete} (astronomer) made a key innovation: instead of working on single equations such as $14x + 15 = 71$, one could now draw conclusions about general ``species'' of equation (linear, quadratic, cubic, etc.) using whole unknowns such as $Ax + B = C$. He ended his 1591 \textit{Introduction} with the statement of purpose: ``TO LEAVE NO PROBLEM UNSOLVED.'' \\
            \noindent \textbf{William Oughtred} pushed this further en 1647. Symbols became a medium free of rhetoric: the naked truth, unadorned, all substance --- exactly what Bacon was after. And so mathematics became thinkable as a means to universal knowledge. \\
            \\
            \textbf{At what cost? The Faustian bargain.} \\
            \noindent The title of the class --- ``from divine perfection to a deal with Satan'' --- captures the tension: mathematics gained its power as a universal language by \emph{incorporating the ``dirty'', practical, merchant algebra} and by \emph{accepting entities} (irrationals, imaginaries) that violated the classical ideals of geometric perfection. As Goethe wrote: ``The alphabet is really now superfluous / for in this sign all men can find salvation.'' \\
            \noindent The cost was accepting numbers and operations that did not correspond to anything in the physical or divine world --- a Faustian bargain for universal explanatory power.
        \subsection*{Class V - 17.03}
            \noindent \textbf{Before the 1870s.} \\
            \noindent The discipline was called \textbf{political economy} and \emph{resembled morals and politics} more than mathematics. Figures like Adam Smith, and John Stuart Mill worked with principles (not laws), specific markets, practical knowledge, and non-mathematical models. Mill even declared in 1848 that the theory was ``complete'' --- nothing left to clear up. \\

            \noindent \textbf{The Crisis of the 1870s.} \\
            \noindent Reality contradicted that supposed ``completeness'': labor protests, strikes, growing national debt, battles over free trade. Clearly, \emph{plenty} remained unresolved.
            
            \noindent The turning point had a name: \textbf{William Stanley Jevons}. He wanted ``to fling aside, once and for ever, the preposterous assumptions of political economy'' and \emph{rebuild the discipline} on Bentham's utilitarianism (humans as creatures who minimize pain and maximize pleasure). His central claim was: ``Economics must be mathematical, simply \emph{because it deals with quantities}''.

            \noindent But, \emph{which} mathematics? The arithmetic of the Cambridge Tripos couldn't handle how tiny amounts of goods produce tiny increases in happiness. The solution came from Leibniz's calculus (the \emph{derivative}), which allowed him to develop the principle of diminishing marginal utility, the bedrock of contemporary economics. \\
            \\
            \noindent \textbf{The cost.} \\
            \noindent Everything that didn't fit into calculus (class struggle, moral economy, land ownership debates) was expelled as ``non-economic knowledge''. Jevons \emph{freed economics from its material constraints} --- no more need to know about farms, ships, or coal. You could now do it at your desk. \\
            \\
            \noindent \textbf{Critical Reception.} \\
            \noindent Even skilled mathematicians like Alfred Marshall were wary: he used mathematics sparingly because he believed a good mathematical theorem on economic hypotheses was \emph{unlikely} to be good economics. \\
            \noindent More recently, Krugman argued economists \emph{mistook mathematical beauty for truth}, and Piketty spoke of a ``childish passion for mathematics'' at the expense of historical research. \\
            \\
            \noindent \textbf{Why Did It Stick?}
            \begin{enumerate}
                \item \textbf{Rites of passage.} Economics students feel they must master formal modeling to compete, even if they doubt its real usefulness (\underline{Spiegler}).
                \item \textbf{Status anxiety.} Unlike engineers or doctors, economists can't point to concrete objects or predictive power; \emph{mathematics makes them ``feel smart'' and valuable}.
                \item \textbf{Authority in a technocratic world.} Mathematics in economics mostly serves to say ``Look at how very scientific I am'' (\underline{McCloskey}).
                \item \textbf{The genie has left the bottle.} Economists \emph{created economic phenomena through mathematics}, and now there's no way of knowing them except through mathematics.
                \item \textbf{A bridge between tech, management, and finance,} plus a quest for truth beyond particulars and idiosyncrasies. 
            \end{enumerate}
            \noindent \textbf{Conclusion.} \\
            \noindent Schabas notes there's no obvious reason economics should ever have adopted mathematics --- but the same could be said of physics, and today's economics is inconceivable without that mathematical anchoring. 

        \subsection*{Class VI - 19.03 (Economics and Physics, the science of all things)}
            \noindent No economics if mathematics didn't exist. Economics wouldn't make sense. \\
            \\
            \noindent What does trade have to do with gravity? ``to flow'' and ``to swell'' also have to do with physics, some words used in businesses are also used in physics! Such as liquidity, flows, pipes, bubbles, and so on. There IS a reason for this. \\
            \\
            \noindent Mathematics is the language of physics, and physics have to do with economics as we can tell. There was an urge to create such a connection, it didn't happen naturally. Some concepts were borrowed from natural sciences. \\
            \\
            \noindent There was an active urge to create this connection: it \emph{didn't happen naturally}. 19th-century economists consciously \emph{borrowed concepts} from the natural sciences to \emph{justify} the use of mathematical reasoning and to ``discover'' general laws of economics. Portraying economics as a natural science was also \emph{an attempt to endow it with more authority}. ``Economics concerns itself with the interplay of a variety of forces, and for this reason it is impossible to present it without the aid of mathematics, just as is the case with astronomy, physics, mechanics and so forth'' (\underline{Gossen}). \\
            \\
            \noindent \textbf{But why physics? Why did economics get such an unhealthy obsession with physics?} \\
            \noindent Why not astronomy, or biology? Because by the end of the 18th century and throughout the 19th, \emph{physics was the queen of sciences}. It had huge discoveries: light, wave theory, atoms, X-rays… it seemed to be \emph{approaching a unified theory of everything}. It was the closest we'd ever felt to explaining everything. 
            \begin{itemize}
                \item \textbf{Fisher.} He studied general equilibrium analysis and built a hydraulic mechanism to simulate equilibrium prices and quantities. He used \emph{fluids as money}, giving flesh to his ideas with mere water and balances. He \emph{explained economics through physical phenomena and mechanisms}. He also used a mechanical balance of exchange to clarify the quantity theory of money.
                \item \textbf{Bill Phillips.} He built the MONIAC: a hydraulic machine. Separate water tanks represented households, business, government, exporting and importing sectors. Coloured water pumped around the system measured income, spending and GDP.                
                \item \textbf{Walras.} In what he called ``a new branch of mathematics'', he linked economics to Newton's laws. He \emph{argued economics was a mathematical science}, exploring the analogy between speed, space and time on one side, and scarcity, utility and quantity on the other.
            \end{itemize}
            \noindent \textbf{Fisher's Translations.} \\
            \noindent There is a direct correlation between mechanical and economic terms: particle/individual, space/commodity, energy/utility... \\
            \noindent This direct correlation is still with us today. \\
            \\
            \noindent \textbf{Jevons's Ideas.}
            \begin{enumerate}
                \item Utility is difficult even to conceive, but it is the amount of utility that prompts us to buy, sell, consume, work, produce, and we perceive it through its quantitative effects (wages, prices, etc.), just as we can no more know or measure gravity in itself, but we measure the motion of a pendulum.
                \item In practice, no market ever long fulfils the theoretical conditions of equilibrium, but every branch of physics involves abstracting from the complexities of the actual world. \emph{So we don't need to know everything in the market to believe in The Market}.
            \end{enumerate}
            \noindent \textbf{Is this purely our imagination?} \\
            \noindent Were economics and physics ever really similar, or have we imagined them and designed them such that now our economic intuition — our economic culture — cannot imagine itself out of it?
        \subsection*{Class VII - 26.03}
            \noindent \textbf{What is a model in business, finance and the economy?} \\
            \\
            Not job division, a blueprint to create a boat, nor a early-dated detailed map: \textit{tableau economique} IS a model, and, to this day, we don't know how it works. It is an economic chart dated 1750. The purpose was to show the king Louis XV how money spread in his kingdom. Why is it a model? You can work WITH it: it is not about the right scale, nor the detail. You could multiply and divide across the \textit{tableau} and figure out how money fluctuated back in the day. It was designed to map out different scenarios - the same exact things models do nowadays. You can enter and formulate hypotheses WITH it. \textbf{If it's not schematic, it does not work as a model. A model is never just a portrait and never work through analogies.} No model can be tweaked in an infinity of ways: it is a \textbf{managed manipulation}.

            Models have to be small enough in scale (simplified) in order to figure out what is essential and innecesary. It needs to be not too small to be trivial and not too big to become overly-generalised. \\
            \\
            \noindent They have two rules:
            \begin{itemize}
                \item \textbf{Formal rules.} Mathematics, algebra, physics.
                \item \textbf{Substance rules.} Economic principles.
            \end{itemize}
            They also have to be bounded and self-contained. Anything you need to know about a certain phenomenon is included, and anything else is out of this model.\\
            \\
            \textbf{How come economists fail SO much and yet we still call them?}\\
            \\
            \noindent For four reasons:
            \begin{itemize}
                \item \textbf{They seek refuge in models.} The market is immense: models focus on a subset of every element in the market. When something unpredictable happens, economists treat as that phenomenon as an exogenous factor. This unknowability is a resource for economists, and they justify their errors with the premise of ``the market is too big to be bounded within a definition''.
                \item \textbf{Forecasts.} Economists tend to stick together in a forecast since it is risky to go all alone in your own forecast. Forecasts were wrong, but no error was made since it is supported by a model.
                \item \textbf{Emphasis on narrative.} Economists usually present actual numbers for forecasting right next to a whole paragraph. A \emph{whole} narrative supports a certain number. These numbers might be wrong, but the narrative was right because it still matches the coming string of events.
                \item \textbf{Emphasis on procedure and compliance.} The last chance to seek refuge when economists are wrong is \emph{compliance} when they are way off from their forecast. So they show that the procedures of forecasting were made exactly how the book told them to. 
            \end{itemize}
            For all these reasons, modelling is a \emph{belief-based practice}: it is not a result-based practice. We use models because we believe in them as a way of knowing the world. They are the right way of doing things, even though they usually fail half the time. \\
            \\
            \noindent Models are to the culture of business as magical tools are to magic. And the narrative resembles the spell, because they \emph{need} to get it right. \\
            So: magic = model, narrative = spell and economist = magician.

        \subsection*{Class IX - 07.04}
            \noindent \textbf{Part (I): Quantification} \\
            \noindent Assigning numbers to things that don't naturally include numbers. Counting apples is not the same thing as counting utility. Numbers do not have to be divine or perfect --- they have to exist, we need a number of acres to inherit or how much wheat we have to sell. Same as how much money we have to pay for something. \\
            \\
            \noindent \underline{Example 1:} numbers are not invented, they are estimated. Like, how on earth can you measure how much it costs per year the noise pollution in terms of heart diseases? \\
            \noindent \underline{Example 2:} how to measure the engagement of students on a subject? \\
            \noindent \underline{Example 3:} rider ratings in Uber. A driver's rating is interpretable for everybody, but there are different conceptions about what a ``good driver'' is. \\
            \noindent \underline{Example 4:} people needed to be compensated after the Chernobyl disaster: cancer, dead cattle, destroyed houses. How do you measure the \emph{damage}? 
            \\
            \noindent Numbers transfer the authority to an argument where numbers are not organically used. It becomes an immensely complicated game, since it is a purely subjective matter. Once numbers are attached to things, we can start making new claims about them. \\
            \\
            \noindent \textbf{Part (II): Audit Cultures} \\
            \noindent More numbers imply moral traction in the key of improvement, accountability and self-responsibility. Now that numbers are included, morality has taken place. \\
            \\
            \noindent Exams had no numbers back in the day: you either passed or not with oral examinations. Universities competed with each other, and Cambridge started taking written test. So a test grade became a number attached to a person. This was set so that the universities could figure out which one was the best. So people would strive to get a 10. You ought to be a ``10'' student: if you were a ``1'' student, it was not desirable. Numbers tell you what you \emph{should} be and what you are at the moment. A moral transfer from the number \emph{to} a person.\\
            \\
            \noindent This matters because it sets targets that you should aim for. The performance says a lot about someone in \emph{moral} terms. A low grade doesn't mean you are a \emph{bad} person, but a \emph{bad student}. \\
            \\
            \noindent \underline{Example (I): Consultants' Billability} Breeds a moral competition between workers because it measures their ``quality'', and their father company is not paying their salary but another company. This morality says something about your quality as a professional, and people \emph{aim} to a certain percent of billability. \\
            \noindent \underline{Example (II) ``Employee Engagement Score'': it is a measure of employees' sentiments, based in a total shareholder return annualised.} \\
            \\
            \noindent Numbers have the advantage that they travel from context to context. Nothing else can do this. Also, they work as a tool for detachment.
